\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Generative modeling has undergone a profound transformation with the advent of diffusion models and their continuous-time counterparts, flow-based models \citep{ho2020denoising, song2020score, lipman2022flow}. These approaches learn a time-dependent velocity field that transports a simple noise distribution to a complex data distribution via an ordinary differential equation (ODE). While they achieve state-of-the-art results in image synthesis, audio generation, and scientific simulation, they share a critical limitation: generating a single sample requires simulating the ODE through tens to hundreds of time steps, each demanding a forward pass of a large neural network. This renders real-time or interactive deployment impractical.

Flow Matching (FM) \citep{lipman2022flow} provides a simulation-free framework for training continuous normalizing flows by regressing against conditional velocity fields. Its straight, optimal-transport trajectories enable faster integration than the curved paths of diffusion models and offer a clean mathematical setting for studying one-step acceleration---the focus of this survey.

This computational bottleneck has spurred extensive work on reducing the NFE budget. Early strategies include caching-based acceleration, which reuses intermediate features across sampling steps; progressive distillation \citep{salimans2022progressive}, which iteratively halves the step count; and consistency models \citep{song2023consistency}, which learn a direct noise-to-data map. Building on these foundations, a family of velocity-based one-step methods has emerged directly within the flow matching framework: consistency flow matching, shortcut models, mean flows, and AlphaFlow. Each takes a distinct approach---enforcing velocity constancy, bootstrapping self-consistency, predicting average velocities, or unifying objectives via curriculum learning---but all share the common aim of replacing iterative ODE simulation with a single network evaluation.

This survey traces the development of one-step flow matching from its conceptual origins to the current state of the art. Our contributions are as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item We provide a unified narrative that connects flow matching, traditional acceleration techniques, consistency models, and modern velocity-based methods within a single mathematical framework.
    \item We analyze four key methodological advances---consistency flow matching, shortcut models, mean flows, and AlphaFlow---comparing their training requirements, theoretical insights, and empirical performance.
    \item We summarize experimental setups and standard baselines used to evaluate one-step models, placing state-of-the-art results in a unified comparative context.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this survey is organized as follows. \Cref{sec:related} reviews related work, including the foundations of flow matching and conditional flow matching (\cref{subsec:fm-cfm}), traditional acceleration strategies such as caching and step distillation (\cref{subsec:traditional}), and consistency models which provide the conceptual foundation for all velocity-based one-step methods (\cref{subsec:consistency-models}). \Cref{sec:onestep} presents the four core families of one-step methods: consistency flow matching, shortcut models, mean flows, and AlphaFlow. \Cref{sec:experiments} describes the experimental setup, datasets, metrics, and baselines. \Cref{sec:results} synthesizes the empirical results. \Cref{sec:conclusion} concludes with future directions.
