\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

Having reviewed the methodological foundations of one-step flow matching, we now turn to the empirical record. Following the AlphaFlow experimental protocol, we compare the three surveyed velocity-based methods---Consistency-FM, MeanFlow, and AlphaFlow---across DiT-B/2, DiT-XL/2, and DiT-XL/2+ scales on class-conditional ImageNet-1K 256$\times$256. MeanFlow and AlphaFlow numbers are taken from \citet{zhang2025alphaflow}; Consistency-FM results are left as placeholders because our runs are not yet complete. We also include representative few-step baselines from the AlphaFlow paper for context.

\textbf{One-step generation.} At the DiT-XL/2+ scale, AlphaFlow achieves the strongest 1-NFE result among the surveyed methods, with an FID of $2.58$ \citep{zhang2025alphaflow}. The same AlphaFlow-XL/2 checkpoint reaches $2.95$ FID after 240 epochs, already outperforming the MeanFlow-XL/2 baseline trained for the same number of epochs ($3.47$ FID). At the smaller DiT-B/2 scale, AlphaFlow reduces the 1-NFE FID from $6.04$ (MeanFlow) to $5.40$, showing consistent gains across model capacities. The Consistency-FM placeholders will allow us to assess whether enforcing velocity consistency directly can match or exceed the average-velocity approach at comparable scale.

\textbf{Few-step generation.} With two function evaluations, AlphaFlow-XL/2+ reaches $2.15$ FID, and AlphaFlow-XL/2 reaches $2.34$ FID, both below the MeanFlow-XL/2 result of $2.46$ FID \citep{zhang2025alphaflow}. The gap is smaller at DiT-B/2 ($5.01$ vs.~$5.17$), suggesting that the curriculum benefit is most pronounced at larger scale. The external FACM-XL/2 baseline reports $2.07$ FID, but uses a more complex training schedule and roughly twice the compute per epoch; AlphaFlow-XL/2+ matches this quality with a simpler curriculum.

\textbf{Training efficiency.} AlphaFlow's curriculum reduces the reliance on expensive border-case ($r=t$) flow-matching supervision compared with MeanFlow, while improving final convergence and sample quality \citep{zhang2025alphaflow}. At DiT-B/2, AlphaFlow achieves better 1-NFE and 2-NFE FID than MeanFlow even with only 25\% border-case flow matching, whereas MeanFlow requires 75\% for its best result. These gains indicate that algorithmic innovations in the training objective---rather than simply scaling compute---are essential for advancing one-step flow matching.

\subsection{Flow-Path Straightness and Transport Cost}

Although FID is the headline metric, the geometric quality of the learned flow---namely the straightness of trajectories and the transport cost---is equally important for understanding why one-step methods succeed. Flow Matching with optimal-transport paths (arXiv:2210.02747) established that straight-line interpolants yield faster training and better generalization than curved diffusion paths \citep{lipman2022flow}. Consistency-FM (arXiv:2407.02398) explicitly measures velocity consistency as a proxy for straightness and shows that enforcing it reduces the number of training steps needed to reach a given FID \citep{zhou2024consistency}. Rectified flow (arXiv:2209.03003, summarized in the Materials README) provides the theoretical grounding: recursively applying rectification yields flows with monotonically non-increasing convex transport costs, and when the flow becomes perfectly straight, a single Euler step gives exact transport. These results confirm that straightness is not merely a geometric curiosity but a practical predictor of sample efficiency.

The empirical landscape reveals two broad strategies: (1) \emph{training from scratch} (MeanFlow, AlphaFlow), which avoids the cost and dependency of a pre-trained teacher but demands careful optimization design; and (2) \emph{constrained training} (Consistency-FM), which imposes additional structure such as velocity consistency or multi-segment decomposition to handle complex distributions. Taken together, the results in \Cref{tab:imagenet-results} suggest that one-step flow matching is no longer a theoretical curiosity: it is a practical, competitive paradigm for high-resolution generative modeling.
