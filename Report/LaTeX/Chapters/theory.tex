\section{Theoretical Analysis}
\label{sec:theory}

The methods presented in \Cref{sec:onestep} are not merely empirical tricks; they are grounded in rigorous mathematical analysis. In this section, we examine the theoretical foundations that guarantee the correctness and convergence of one-step flow matching.

\subsection{Error Bounds and Wasserstein Control}

A central theoretical question is: if we approximate the true flow map or velocity field with a neural network, how close is the generated distribution to the true data distribution? Several of the methods discussed provide explicit bounds.

For the distillation objectives in AYF, both the Eulerian and Lagrangian map distillation losses control the $W_2$ distance between the teacher and student distributions. Specifically, the Wasserstein-2 error is bounded by an exponential of the integral of the Lipschitz constant of the velocity field, multiplied by the distillation loss:
\begin{equation}
    W_2^2(\rho_1, \hat{\rho}_1) \leq e^{1 + 2\int |C_t| dt} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{distill}}(\hat{X}),
\end{equation}
where $C_t$ is the Lipschitz constant of the velocity field at time $t$. This means that minimizing the distillation loss directly minimizes the distributional error.

Terminal Velocity Matching provides a similar but distinct guarantee. Under the assumption that the learned velocity field $u_\theta(\cdot, s)$ is Lipschitz continuous with constant $L(s)$, the TVM objective upper-bounds the 2-Wasserstein distance between the data distribution and the model's pushforward distribution:
\begin{equation}
    W_2^2(f_{t \to 0}^\theta \# p_t, p_0) \leq \int_0^t \lambda[L](s) \, \mathcal{L}_{\text{TVM}}^{t,s}(\theta) \, ds + C,
\end{equation}
where $\lambda[\cdot]$ is a functional of the Lipschitz constants and $C$ is a non-optimizable constant. This is a powerful result because it connects the per-time training objective directly to a global measure of generative quality.

\subsection{Unification Under the Flow Map Formalism}

The flow map framework provides a unified lens through which to view all one-step methods. The flow map $X_{s,t}$ is the integral operator that transports solutions of the probability flow ODE from time $s$ to time $t$. It satisfies the semigroup property $X_{t,\tau}(X_{s,t}(x)) = X_{s,\tau}(x)$ and the tangent condition $\lim_{s \to u} \partial_u X_{s,u}(x) = v_{u,u}(x) = b_u(x)$.

Within this framework:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Standard Flow Matching} corresponds to learning the instantaneous velocity $v_{u,u}(x)$, i.e., the flow map in the infinitesimal limit.
    \item \textbf{Consistency Models} correspond to learning the flow map with $s=0$ (the endpoint map), collapsing the full trajectory to a single prediction.
    \item \textbf{Consistency Trajectory Models} and \textbf{Mean Flows} correspond to learning the flow map for arbitrary $s, t$ but with specific parameterizations of the average velocity.
    \item \textbf{AYF} and \textbf{TVM} are direct attempts to learn the general flow map $X_{s,t}$ for all time pairs, with additional theoretical guarantees on distributional error.
\end{itemize}

This unification reveals that the apparent diversity of methods in the literature is in fact a progression along a single mathematical axis: the scope of the time interval over which the flow map is learned.

\subsection{Limitations of Consistency Models}

AYF provides a rigorous explanation for why standard consistency models degrade in multi-step sampling. Consider a Gaussian data distribution $p_{\text{data}}(x) = \mathcal{N}(0, c^2 I)$. The optimal consistency model $f^*(x_t, t)$ maps any noisy input directly to the clean mean. However, for any $\delta > 0$, there exists a suboptimal consistency model $f(x_t, t)$ with error less than $\delta$ at every time, yet whose multi-step sampling error increases without bound as the number of steps grows. This is because the consistency objective forces the model to predict clean outputs from all noise levels, and any small error in this prediction is amplified when the model is applied sequentially.

Flow maps avoid this by learning the general transport $X_{s,t}$ rather than the endpoint map $X_{t,0}$, allowing them to compose steps without error accumulation.
